Reading coverage ratios
Both metrics measure different things and both should be strong. DSCR answers "can the business afford its debt payments?" by comparing operating earnings against total debt service (principal + interest). Interest Coverage answers "can the business afford its interest?" by comparing operating earnings only against interest expense (excluding principal). DSCR matters more for cash management and covenant testing; interest coverage matters more for credit risk assessment. At 2.74x DSCR on the most conservative EBITDA lens, debt service is well-covered with significant cushion. At 4.33x interest coverage, interest payments are not a meaningful constraint on operations. These ratios position the company comfortably for additional borrowing capacity if a strategic opportunity (e.g., cap-lift store expansion) called for it.

Reading the blended view
Headline term-debt rate is 10.20% weighted-average — driven by the 15% F&F notes and the 13% Sky note, partially offset by three 0%–3% facilities. When trade payables (AP) are included as a real source of operating financing, the blended cost drops to approximately 2.85%, reflecting that more than two-thirds of total liabilities are 0%-effective-rate. This is not free money in any literal sense — extended AP carries vendor-relationship costs, supply-chain risk, and reputational considerations — but it does reflect the true effective cost of operating with the current capital structure. The metric is most useful for comparison: against a benchmark cost of new capital (a hypothetical bank line might price at 8–12% in this industry, an MSO acquirer's cost of capital might be 5–7%, an investor note might be 12–18%), the current blended cost sits at the low end. The high implied "subsidy" from AP also explains why aggressive AP discipline matters — the day AP normalizes to industry levels, the blended cost rises toward the term-debt figure.

The Discipline

How AP aging is actively managed week-to-week

AP management is integrated directly into the weekly cashflow tracking system. Each week, three layers are reviewed and reconciled: (1) weekly product incoming — what is flowing in from vendors and at what cost; (2) weekly payment commitments — what is being paid out to which vendors and in what priority; (3) category movement — whether vendors are moving in favorable directions (Hold → Payment Plan → Core) or unfavorable ones. Vendor relationships, supply continuity, and aged-balance reduction are all balanced against the operating cash position week by week. The integrated tracker (separate working spreadsheet titled "Sandbox NECC AP + Budget") is the operational source of truth; this dashboard reports the resulting balances and trajectory.

Reading AP aging honestly · two lenses, two different stories
AP aging at this scale is a real liability and a real ongoing challenge — not a number to minimize or hide. Standard 30/60/90 aging shows how long dollars have been outstanding, regardless of vendor relationship or payment plan status. The 91+ bucket of $768K as of Jan 31 looks alarming under this lens. The internal risk-lens categorization tells a different story: most of those aged dollars sit with Brick or Core vendors operating on agreed terms, or with Payment Plan vendors in a managed paydown — which is "old debt that won't or shouldn't be a problem" rather than "old debt that signals trouble." The Hold-category portion is the genuinely stressed segment and the one that warrants the most management attention. Both lenses are necessary: 30/60/90 is the format an outside reader (auditor, lender, buyer's diligence) will recognize and ask about; the risk-lens is the format that actually drives weekly management decisions. The dashboard shows both because telling the AP story honestly requires both. Three signals the discipline is working: total balance trending down across consecutive months, Hold-category dollars shrinking, vendor categories migrating in favorable directions (Hold → Payment Plan → Core/Brick). Three signals of trouble: total balance growing, Hold-category dollars accelerating or new Hold vendors emerging, vendor supply disruptions. AP management belongs in the deleveraging narrative because it is the same discipline applied to a different liability category — the company is reducing both formal debt and trade payables in parallel, and reporting both honestly is what makes the dashboard credible to anyone reading it.

AP aging — internal risk-lens categorization. The operational tracker (Sandbox NECC AP + Budget) groups AP into four vendor categories rather than 30/60/90 buckets: Brick Vendors (core product vendors with agreed weekly payment cadence — current), Core Vendors (recurring service/product vendors on standard terms — current), Payment Plan Vendors (past-due balances on agreed paydown plans — being managed), and Hold Vendors (past-due balances without active payment plans, often disputed or paused — watch list). This grouping matches how management actually makes decisions: Brick + Core are "won't be a problem," Payment Plan is "being managed," Hold is "could become a problem." Per-category balances and vendor counts populate from data/ap-aging-data.js, which is updated when the operational tracker is refreshed. When category balances are null in the data file, the dashboard renders a "pending" indicator rather than a fabricated value.

Operational management mechanism. Tracker drives category-movement decisions (e.g., negotiating a Hold vendor onto a Payment Plan, or transitioning a Payment Plan vendor back to Core terms after paydown completes).
