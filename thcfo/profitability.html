471(c) Reconciliation: Book vs. Economic P&L

Your books capitalize labor, overhead, marketing, security, and other operating costs into inventory under §471(c), so they flow through COGS rather than appearing as below-the-line operating expenses. The "Economic" view reclassifies these to where they would sit in a normal company P&L. Both views produce identical Operating Income — only the geography of the costs differs.

Reading the reconciliation
Both views describe the same business and produce identical operating income — the difference is where costs sit on the page. The book view shows costs as the §471(c) tax method classifies them (mostly inside COGS via inventoriable cost), which compresses reported gross margin to 11.8%. The economic view reclassifies operating costs back to where they sit on a typical retailer's P&L, producing a ~60% gross margin in line with vertically integrated cannabis operators. The 11.8% figure is not a sign of operational weakness; it is the geographic consequence of an aggressive but defensible inventory-capitalization tax strategy. Anyone evaluating the business at the gross-margin line should be reading the economic view, not the book view.

Reading store contribution margin
Direct contribution margin is the cleanest single answer to whether each store is making money on its own merits — it strips out shared overhead and shows what each location produces from revenue minus directly-attributable costs. Pepperell's contribution rate (~27%) is the highest across the portfolio despite generating fewer absolute dollars than Dracut, which reflects a smaller cost base (less square footage, smaller team) more than higher pricing. Dracut's $1.08M direct contribution reflects scale — it carries the largest cost base but generates more than 60% of total contribution. Groton's negative direct contribution is typical for new cannabis retail in the first 12–18 months; the location is in ramp phase and the projected full-year run-rate at $750K revenue points to break-even or modestly positive. Corporate and shared overhead (HQ payroll, marketing, professional fees, IT, insurance, etc.) consumes ~$890K below the store-direct lines.

Reading the bridge
EBITDA is a measure of operating earnings before financial-structure noise (interest, taxes) and non-cash accounting charges (depreciation, amortization). For a §471(c) operator, two reclassifications apply before standard add-backs: loan interest sitting inside COGS via inventoriable cost gets reclassed back to where it normally lives, and depreciation that flows through inventory rather than as a separate P&L line gets surfaced. Adjusted EBITDA then layers on non-recurring costs and strategic-buyer synergies. Two inputs are working estimates pending finalization: the 2025 depreciation and amortization schedule (currently estimated, likely understated given recent buildouts) and the line-item categorization of one-time costs.

Reading year-over-year
Revenue grew modestly (+3%) while profitability nearly tripled. Three drivers: full vertical integration (MACC consolidated from Jan 1, 2025, eliminating arms-length transfer pricing distortions on the prior-year books), accelerated debt repayment freeing operating cash (institutional debt paid down ~$750K, replaced with friendlier investor-note structure), and improved unit economics across mature stores. In a Massachusetts cannabis market that experienced sector-wide wholesale price compression during this period, holding revenue flat-to-modestly-positive is itself a directional positive — peer operators saw double-digit revenue declines. Margin expansion from 4.6% to 11.8% gross at the book level is the more important signal.

Reading ROIC
ROIC measures how productive each dollar of invested capital has been at generating direct contribution margin. Blended retail ROIC of ~61% is high in any retail context — typical brick-and-mortar retail trades in the 15–30% range. Two factors drive cannabis retail ROIC higher: gross margins on regulated product (the markup from wholesale to retail is structurally larger than commodity retail), and capital intensity is mostly buildout cost rather than building ownership (stores are leased, so invested capital is bounded by leasehold improvements and startup costs). The ROIC framework is most useful for capital-allocation decisions — comparing the return on a new store buildout to alternatives (farm expansion, manufacturing capacity, debt paydown, M&A). Vertical integration (own-grown product flowing through retail at consolidated cost) further improves the contribution margin per store; the Verticalization page quantifies that spread.

Methodology Notes & Sources

Source data. 2025 P&L by month from QuickBooks accrual basis (post-monthly-close), 2025 year-end balance sheet, 2024 standalone P&L for NECC (pre-MACC consolidation). All figures reflect the company's monthly close cycle; pre-close data is excluded from this dashboard.

Book P&L. As filed in QuickBooks. §471(c) inventory capitalization rolls labor, marketing, rent, security, insurance, software, loan interest, and most other operating costs into inventoriable cost-of-goods-sold. Below-the-line operating expenses are negligible by design (~$19K) — almost everything sits inside COGS until inventory turns.

Economic P&L reconciliation. "Product COGS only" is computed as the sum of: 65001 Products-Cannabis ($3,462,074) + 65002 Products-Non-Cannabis ($89,095). Reclassified opex is the difference between book COGS and product COGS only ($7,768,336 - $3,551,169 = $4,236,430), which we then itemize back to typical retailer-P&L categories. Operating income is unchanged across both views — only the geography of the costs shifts.

Store contribution margin (modeled). Revenue per store from the onboarding questionnaire ($5.05M Dracut / $2.50M Pep / $0.54M Groton / $0.72M Colrain). Product COGS per store allocated by retail revenue share at 43.9% gross-cost ratio. Store payroll allocated from cashflow doc weekly payroll lines (Dracut ~$55K/wk × 26 = $1.43M; Pep ~$22K/wk × 26 = $572K; Groton ~$8K/wk × 26 = $208K). Rent from cashflow doc and lease schedule (Dracut $14K/mo, Pep $6.6K/mo, Groton $8.9K/mo). "Store-level Other" estimated based on local utilities, security, and incidentals. This is a triangulated model, not booked numbers — QB class attribution is unreliable across stores, particularly for shared expenses like marketing.

Corporate & shared overhead. Computed as a residual: total book COGS + below-line opex minus directly-attributable store costs minus farm costs. Approximately $890K. Includes HQ payroll (executives, admin), shared marketing, professional fees, IT/software, insurance, HQ security, dues, training, etc.

Farm contribution. Estimated as ~$240K — preliminary number pending the Verticalization page build, which will model farm economics including transfer pricing, capacity utilization, wholesale margin, and standalone valuation.

EBITDA bridge. Starts from book NOI ($1,022,662). Adds back loan interest of $236,360 (line 65240 in 2025 P&L; this interest sits inside COGS via 471(c) capitalization). Adds estimated D&A of $50,000 (working estimate — to be replaced by the formal 2025 depreciation schedule from the company's tax accountant, which will be the single largest input update). The Strategic lens then adds back $659K of categorized synergies (configured on the Valuation page synergy section). Result: $1,968,186 Strategic Adjusted EBITDA — Tree House internal calculation.

ROIC by store. Invested capital per store = Startup Costs (from balance sheet line items: 15800 Dracut $317K, 15900 Pep $72K, 15010 Groton $543K + $88K rents) plus an estimated allocated share of Leasehold Improvements (total $1.55M; allocated 50/30/20 roughly to Groton/Dracut/Pep based on size and recency of buildout) plus a working-capital share. Direct contribution margin from the modeled store P&Ls above. ROIC = Direct Contribution / Invested Capital.

YoY caveats. 2024 figures are NECC standalone (pre-MACC consolidation). MACC's $971K of 2024 revenue and $157K of net operating income are not included in the 2024 NECC numbers. From 2025 onward, the entities are fully integrated. A clean apples-to-apples comparison of consolidated 2024 vs. consolidated 2025 would add MACC pro-forma to 2024 — a refinement worth doing in a future iteration but not material to the directional story.

Pending inputs. 2025 depreciation and amortization schedule from the company's tax accountant (the single largest open item). Line-item categorization of one-time costs and synergy add-backs in a supporting workpaper. Both will be incorporated when produced.
