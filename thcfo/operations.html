Reading product mix
Vape, flower, edibles, and pre-rolls together account for ~83% of product cost and represent the core revenue drivers. The MACC line (in-house, 12.3% of product cost) reflects own-grown product flowing through retail at consolidated cost — economically larger than this line shows because pricing applied to that product at the retail counter generates higher gross margin than equivalent third-party product. Concentrates, topicals, and accessories are smaller categories but represent margin-expansion opportunities if buyer demand grows. The September 2026 manufacturing license activation will allow Tree House to internalize pre-roll and infused-pre-roll production, which today is sourced as third-party COGS.

Reading geography
New Hampshire customers represent over half of scanned traffic across the portfolio — a critical strategic dependency. NH legalization (which has been discussed periodically in the NH legislature) would meaningfully reduce cross-border demand for MA dispensaries near the border. Conversely, until NH legalizes, Tree House's border-proximate locations (especially Pepperell and Groton) capture an out-of-state demand pool that pure-MA-resident operators do not. The Massachusetts customer base (~43%) is the durable local foundation. The dependency on NH cross-border traffic should be tracked over time as a directional risk factor.

§471(c) and the Tax Lens

The single most important tax fact about Tree House: cannabis tax accounting is governed by §471(c) of the Internal Revenue Code, not §280E. This explainer covers what §471(c) does, why it matters, and what changes for a buyer who cannot continue the strategy.

What §471(c) Does

A small-business inventory accounting method available under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act

§280E is the federal tax provision that prevents most cannabis businesses from deducting ordinary operating expenses (wages, rent, marketing, etc.) for federal tax purposes. Only Cost of Goods Sold is deductible under §280E.

§471(c), added by the TCJA, allows certain small businesses with gross receipts under approximately $30M to use a simplified inventory accounting method that conforms inventory to the company's books or applies a simplified rule. Cannabis tax practitioners have used §471(c) to capitalize a much broader category of operating costs into inventory cost — labor, utilities, rent attributable to production, and overhead — which then flow through Cost of Goods Sold when product sells. Because §280E permits COGS deduction even when ordinary expenses are not deductible, §471(c) effectively converts otherwise-non-deductible operating expenses into deductible cost-of-goods.

Buyer-Type Tax Implications

Whether a buyer can continue the §471(c) strategy depends on size and structure

Sub-$30M Acquirer

A buyer below the §471(c) gross-receipts threshold can continue the inventory method post-close. Tax-affected EBITDA roughly preserves what the books show. Strategic-buyer category for valuation purposes.

MSO / Large Acquirer (above threshold)

A buyer above the §471(c) threshold cannot continue this method on the acquired entity. Post-close, the operations would be subject to §280E in the traditional way: only COGS deductible, ordinary opex non-deductible federally. EBITDA the buyer underwrites is structurally lower than the EBITDA Tree House currently generates.

Reading the tax lens
The §471(c) strategy is one of the most consequential CFO decisions reflected on this dashboard. It produces three downstream effects: (1) reported gross margins look low (~12% book) because operating costs are capitalized into COGS — the economic reconciliation on the Profitability page corrects this view; (2) effective federal tax is near zero, freeing operating cash for debt reduction and reinvestment; (3) the strategy does not transfer to a buyer above the $30M threshold, which is itself a legitimate argument for revenue-multiple framing in any valuation conversation rather than EBITDA-multiple framing — revenue is buyer-agnostic, while EBITDA shifts depending on which tax method the buyer can use post-close. The strategy is supported by professional tax counsel and has been employed for three consecutive fiscal years.
