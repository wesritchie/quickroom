Reading the cash flow statement
The cash flow statement reconciles three different views of the business that the P&L alone cannot show. Operating Activities answers "did the core business produce cash?" — book net income gets adjusted for non-cash items and working-capital changes. Investing Activities shows long-term capital deployment — buildouts, equipment, asset purchases. Financing Activities shows movement of debt and equity capital. The 2025 picture: operations generated $551K, capex consumed $45K, financing consumed $514K (net of new investor notes against retired institutional debt), and cash ended essentially flat. The headline of the year is the deleveraging story — five institutional loans (Cobalt, Cromwell, EBF, FinCoast, Sky) were paid down by ~$877K combined while seven friendlier investor notes added $381K, producing a net debt reduction of nearly half a million dollars while keeping the cash balance stable.

Reading the conversion gap
Book profitability and operating cash diverge when balance-sheet items move during the period. The single largest driver in 2025 was a $556K cannabis inventory build — inventory grew from $914K to $1.47M as the cultivation operation ramped and own-grown product accumulated for sell-through. This is intentional: inventory build is a normal feature of vertical integration coming online, and the cost capitalization is part of the §471(c) tax strategy. A $162K AR build and an $11K tips-payable swing also consumed cash, partially offset by sales-tax timing ($128K), vendor-terms extension ($50K), and intercompany adjustments. Net result: 54% cash conversion of book NI. Conversion in the 50–70% range is normal for retailers in inventory-build mode; full-conversion (90%+) typically happens only in steady-state inventory years. The metric to watch in 2026: as inventory normalizes, conversion should rise toward the higher end of that range.

Reading the cash cycle
A negative CCC means cash is collected from customers faster than it must be paid out to vendors — the business effectively runs on supplier credit. For retailers this is a structural advantage; the most extreme examples (large grocery chains) operate on CCC as low as -30 days. Tree House sits at -4 days on the book basis and -17 days on the economic basis — both healthy, with the economic-basis figure more reflective of the underlying operating reality given §471(c) accounting. The metrics to monitor over time are: DIO trending up (signals slow inventory turn or aging stock) and DPO trending down (signals tighter vendor terms or stretched supplier relationships). Both are leading indicators of cash strain before the bank balance shows it.

Reading variance
Once a budget is in place, monthly variance reporting becomes the primary management discipline. Favorable variances (actuals better than budget) get celebrated and analyzed for replicability; unfavorable variances trigger investigation and corrective action. The CFO function is to flag variance early — before three months of compounding — and to maintain credibility of the budget so that variance analysis is treated seriously by the operating team. Even a directional budget for 2026 is better than no budget at all.
Methodology Notes & Sources

Cash position. Account-level balances from 2025 year-end balance sheet. Refresh after each weekly cashflow review. Account purposes documented inline; "store revenue accounts" are daily-deposit pass-through accounts that empty into the BayCoast Operating account on a regular cycle.

Statement of Cash Flows. Generated from 2025 close per QuickBooks. Operating activities use the indirect method (net income reconciled via working-capital adjustments). Investing reflects the tail of Groton startup spending (the store opened February 2025) plus minor leasehold improvements. Financing decomposes the deleveraging story: institutional debt fully retired or reduced, replaced in part by friendlier investor notes.

Days Cash on Hand. Computed as cash on hand divided by daily operating expenses. Daily opex = book COGS + below-line opex ÷ 365 ≈ $21.3K/day. $354K ÷ $21.3K ≈ 17 days. Conventionally, 30–60 days cash on hand is considered comfortable for a retailer; under 30 days requires close cash management.

Cash conversion cycle. CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO. Computed both on book-COGS basis (as the books read) and on product-COGS-only basis (industry-comparable). The book basis understates exposure because §471(c) inflates COGS with capitalized opex. Both views are shown for transparency; the economic basis is the more relevant single measure for benchmarking against peer retailers.

Forward cashflow. Detailed weekly projections through end of 2027 are maintained in a separate working spreadsheet outside this dashboard. Summary point estimates shown here are illustrative; the underlying tracker is the source of truth. Future iteration: direct ingestion of that spreadsheet.

Budget vs. actuals. A formal 2026 P&L budget has not yet been adopted at the time of this dashboard build. Once adopted, the variance table on this page will populate. Recommended budget structure: revenue by store + farm wholesale, COGS at product-only level, opex by category (payroll, marketing, professional, software, insurance, security, rent, utilities, other), capital expenditure, debt service.
